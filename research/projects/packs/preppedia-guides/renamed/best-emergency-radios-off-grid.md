## What to look for (buying checklist)

### 1) Power options that match your scenario
A good emergency radio should survive *boring reality*, not just an imaginary crisis.
- **Primary:** replaceable AA/AAA or a good rechargeable pack
- **Backup:** hand‑crank and/or solar
- **Bonus:** USB charging as a power bank (useful, but don’t over‑trust it)

### 2) Weather alerts (NOAA) & alert behavior
If you’re in the US, NOAA weather alerts are a major reason to own an emergency radio.
Look for:
- alert mode that can wake you,
- volume control,
- and clear instructions.

### 3) Usability in the dark
In real outages, you’ll use it when you’re tired and the lights are out.
Prefer:
- clear buttons,
- a readable display,
- and simple tuning.

### 4) Audio clarity
A radio that’s technically “working” but impossible to hear is a failure.
If it’s for home, consider a model with better speaker output.

### 5) Durability and storage
If you’ll keep it in a go‑bag or vehicle:
- consider temperature swings,
- battery leakage prevention,
- and a storage pouch.

## Recommended off‑grid setup (minimal but smart)

For most households:
1) **One primary emergency radio** (hand‑crank + battery)
2) **Spare batteries** (stored properly)
3) **Written frequency notes** (local info, NOAA channels if relevant)

For a go‑bag:
1) **Compact radio**
2) Backup battery/power plan

## Common mistakes

1) **Buying features you’ll never use**
A simple, reliable radio beats a complicated one you don’t practice with.

2) **No battery plan**
Don’t assume solar/hand‑crank replaces batteries. It’s backup, not magic.

3) **Never testing it**
Set it up once, learn the controls, and store it ready.

## FAQ

### 1) Do I need a hand‑crank radio?
It’s a great backup power option. Most people still rely on batteries as primary.

### 2) What’s the difference between an emergency radio and a normal AM/FM radio?
Emergency radios focus on multiple power options, alerts, and durability.

### 3) Can an emergency radio replace a satellite communicator?
No—different tool. Radios receive information; satellite messengers can send out.

### 4) Should I buy one for every family member?
Usually no. One solid home unit + one compact unit for a go‑bag is a practical start.
